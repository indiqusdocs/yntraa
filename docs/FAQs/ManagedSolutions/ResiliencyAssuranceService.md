# Resiliency Assurance Service


<details><summary>**What is a Disaster?**</summary>Disaster is a natural or man-made (or technological) hazard resulting in an event of substantial extent causing significant physical damage or destruction or loss.</details>

<details><summary>**What is disaster recovery?**</summary>Disaster recovery is the set of procedures, tools, policies, and resources that are collectively intended to revive IT infrastructure and resume critical business functions after a natural or human-triggered disaster - such as a cyberattack, hardware failure, accidental data deletion, etc.</details>

<details><summary>**What is disaster management?**</summary>Disaster management (or emergency management) is the discipline of dealing with and avoiding both natural and manmade disasters.</details>

<details><summary>**What is the difference between an emergency plan vs disaster recovery vs business continuity?**</summary>**Emergency plannings** are those procedures and steps done immediately after an interruption to business. **Disaster recovery** is the steps taken to restore some IT related functions so that some level of services can be offered. A **DR plan** is usually limited in scope to a set of defined IT systems and infrastructure, with the goal being the complete recovery of those systems and infrastructure within a defined timeframe and with minimal data loss. 
**Business continuity** is restoration planning, completing the full circle to get your organisation back to where it was before an interruption. **BC plan** scopes the enterprise, with the goal being the recovery of mission-critical / core business functions to ensure the survival of the enterprise. The business functions to be recovered in BC extend beyond IT systems.</details>

<details><summary>**Who in the organisation should be responsible for creating the plan?**</summary>Assign a team to help and create the plan. While small organizations may be able to get by with one person doing the work, larger organizations will have to enlist the assistance of others, particularly in coordinating various departments to provide needed portions.</details>

<details><summary>**I am taking Backup and Archiving my data. Is this not enough for my DR?**</summary>While the two functions are related, backup is focused on protecting data with the intention of it being used for recovery to a given point in time (the RPO), and archiving is aimed at preserving the state of data or an application for possible future use.
They may sound similar, but they differ in retention cycles and in the frequency or interval at which 
backups are made versus archives. Archives are usually retained for longer periods, such as years, while backups are typically retained for days, weeks, or months.
This is not enough, due to these few major reasons, like Stringent RPO & RTO requirement, Continuous availability of application & its performance as well as Automated recovery & Reverse replication.</details>

<details><summary>**What RPO, RTO & MTPOD means in the context of disaster recovery?**</summary>**RPO** - Recovery point objective (Acceptable loss in a disaster situation). Maximum data loss, i.e., the minimum amount of data that needs to be restored.
**RTO** - Recovery time objective (Lead time to start the service). The pre-determined time at which an activity must be resumed, or resources must be recovered.
**MTPOD** – Maximum tolerable period of disruption (Outer limit of the unavailability).</details>

<details><summary>**What is the difference between synchronous and Asynchronous replication?**</summary>**Synchronous** – Every transaction on the primary source server happens virtually at the same time on the target server. This form of replication is distance sensitive. This works less than equal to 20 KMs of distance.
**Asynchronous** – Replication that allows for “latency” between transactions and replication (typically seconds to minutes).</details>

<details><summary>**What is the difference between Failover and failback?**</summary>**Failover** - Is the capability to switch over to a redundant or standby server or system upon the termination of an existing asset.
**Failback / Switch over** - Back to its original state.</details>

<details><summary>**Why should we test our DR system?**</summary>Testing helps ensure that the DR site meets your current business needs. We often find that firms grow, evolve, and change their production environments as they develop and expand their businesses. In turn, the DR site needs to evolve and change simultaneously to continuously meet business requirements. By regularly testing their DR sites, firms can ensure all of these needs are met and they will be fully prepared to continue operations should a disaster occur.</details>

<details><summary>**How often should we test our DR site?**</summary>We recommend minimum 1 No of testing or drill per annum. More than this 1 no of frequency is always better. Disaster recovery drill is performed to test the section of your disaster recovery plan that details your response to a disaster. By following the exact steps in the disaster recovery plan and verifying that your disaster recovery site is functioning and can provide the required business continuity within the required RPOs and RTOs, you can confirm that this would also be the case if a real disaster occurs. Organizations determine the frequency of disaster recovery drills based on multiple factors, such as requirements by compliance certifications and the cost of each drill for the organization.</details>

<details><summary>**Who should consider outsourcing DR?**</summary>In the past, it's been confined to people with very expensive mainframes. But, in recent years, we've seen a wide variety of our clients looking at outsourcing disaster recovery (DR).
<br/>• Those who want to get a solid handle on their IT investments and cost.<br/><br/>• People who don't know that much about DR and want to have someone else look after it for them.<br/><br/>• People who are looking at the worst-case scenario, perhaps their staff might not survive the disaster.<br/><br/>• Someone who does not have complete capability or skills to manage as well as optimised the cost of DR<br/>. Once you've designed your DR plan it's probably worthwhile looking at the cost of outsourcing vs. doing it in-house. It is also important to consider the time it would take to maintain an in-house DR plan vs. outsourcing.</details>
